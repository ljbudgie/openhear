## Study title

**Pilot evaluation of the OpenHear sovereign DSP pipeline against
participants' own commercially fitted hearing aids.**

(Short title: *OpenHear Pilot v1*.)

## What is this study?

We are testing whether a free, open-source hearing-aid software
called **OpenHear** can deliver speech-in-noise performance
comparable to your own hearing aids, when it uses the same audiogram
your audiologist already produced for you.

We are *not* asking you to give up or change your own hearing aids.
We are asking you to come to two short sessions in which you will
listen to standard speech-in-noise tests through (1) your own
hearing aids and (2) the OpenHear research system, in a randomised
order. You will then answer some questionnaires and tell us which
you preferred.

## Is OpenHear a medical device?

**No.** OpenHear is an open-source research platform. It is not
certified as a medical device and is not a substitute for clinical
care. Your usual hearing aids and your usual audiology care are not
affected by taking part in this study.

## What will happen to my data?

This study is built on a principle we call **data sovereignty**:
*your audiogram is a measurement of your body and it belongs to
you*.

In practice, this means:

1. At the end of your final session, you will be given a USB stick
   containing **all of your raw data** (audiogram, OpenHear fitting,
   QuickSIN responses, questionnaire answers). The data is signed
   with a cryptographic checksum so you, or anyone you share it with,
   can verify it has not been altered.
2. The research team keeps **only an anonymised copy** of your data,
   used solely for the analyses described in the published study
   protocol. We do not store your raw data in any cloud service.
3. If you withdraw from the study at any time, your raw data
   remains yours; the anonymised copy will be deleted unless you
   specifically permit us to keep it for analysis.
4. Anonymised study results will be published openly so that other
   researchers and the public can inspect them. Your name and
   contact details will never be published.

## What are the risks?

* The OpenHear system plays sound into a small ear-worn receiver,
  similar to a hearing aid. The output level is automatically
  limited so that it cannot exceed a safe maximum derived from your
  own audiogram. You will also be given a clearly-marked **mute
  button** that you can press at any time to silence the device.
* Some participants experience temporary mild ear fatigue or
  discomfort after a session of hearing tests. This is not specific
  to OpenHear and is the same risk as a routine clinical hearing
  test.
* As with any new device, there is a small chance of an unexpected
  loud sound. The output limiter is the primary safeguard; the mute
  button is the backup; the study audiologist is present throughout.
* If any wristband or haptic accessory is used alongside the study,
  there is a small chance of skin irritation, thermal warmth, or
  pulses that feel too strong; tell the audiologist immediately so
  the device can be removed and the event recorded.
* If at any point you suspect that your data has not been handled
  as described above (for example a lost USB stick or an unexpected
  copy of your data), tell the audiologist — this is recorded as a
  privacy event and reviewed by the Chief Investigator.
* If you feel a critical sound (such as an alarm or a named voice)
  was missed or misidentified by the system in a way that worries
  you, tell the audiologist — this is recorded as a
  misclassification event.
* No serious risk to your health is anticipated. Any unexpected
  problem will be reported to the Ethics Committee per standard
  research practice.

## What are the benefits?

* You will receive a £XX honorarium and your travel costs
  reimbursed (amount and policy to be confirmed by the partner site).
* You will help establish whether sovereign, open-source hearing
  aid software is a viable alternative to closed, commercial systems
  for people like you.
* You leave with a portable, signed copy of your own audiogram and
  fitting data that you can take to any future clinician or open
  hearing platform.

There is no guaranteed personal benefit to your hearing.

## Voluntary participation and withdrawal

Taking part is entirely voluntary. You can refuse to take part, or
withdraw at any time without giving a reason and without affecting
your medical care or your relationship with your audiology clinic.
