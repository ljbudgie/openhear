## The Doctrine of Audiological Data Sovereignty

*A framework for the lawful assertion of ownership over biometric hearing data.*

## Preamble

When a person sits in an audiological booth and has their hearing measured, something specific happens at a biological level. An audiologist presents tones at calibrated frequencies and intensities. The patient raises their hand when they hear the tone. The audiologist records the threshold — the precise decibel level at which that individual's auditory nerve fires.

That threshold is not a product. It is not intellectual property. It is not a clinical record belonging to a healthcare institution. It is a measurement of a biological fact about a specific human being's nervous system.

It belongs to that human being.

This document asserts that principle — clearly, completely, and without qualification — and applies it to every form of audiological data generated in the course of hearing healthcare: audiograms, fitting profiles, compression parameters, noise reduction settings, and the complete stored state of any hearing aid device worn by a patient.

## Part I — What audiological data actually is

### 1.1 The audiogram

An audiogram is a graph of hearing thresholds across frequencies, typically 250Hz to 8000Hz, measured in decibels Hearing Level (dBHL). It represents the minimum sound pressure level at which a person can detect a pure tone at each frequency.

This data is:
- Generated by the patient's own biological response
- Unique to the patient's anatomy and neurology
- A direct measurement of the patient's body
- Unchangeable by anyone other than the passage of time or medical intervention

No audiologist creates this data. They measure it. The distinction is fundamental. A person who measures a river's depth does not own the river.

### 1.2 The fitting profile

A hearing aid fitting profile is a set of parameters applied to the digital signal processor (DSP) inside a hearing aid. It typically includes:

- Gain values across frequency channels (how much amplification to apply at each frequency)
- Compression ratios and knee points (how the aid responds to loud sounds)
- Maximum Power Output (MPO) limits
- Noise reduction algorithm settings
- Directional microphone (beamforming) configuration
- Programme assignments and automatic scene-switching thresholds

This profile is generated by an audiologist using proprietary fitting software (Phonak Target, Signia Connexx, etc.) and is based entirely on the patient's audiogram. It exists to make the patient's hearing better. It is stored inside a device worn inside or behind the patient's ear.

The fitting profile belongs to the patient.

### 1.3 The stored device state

Modern hearing aids store their complete operational state in onboard memory. This includes the fitting profile, usage logs, environment classification history, streaming preferences, and in some cases physiological data such as steps taken and heart rate approximations.

All of this data was generated by the patient's body, behaviour, and environment. None of it was created by the manufacturer. All of it is stored in a device the patient purchased.

The stored device state belongs to the patient.

## Part II — The lock and who benefits from it

### 2.1 The proprietary lock

Hearing aid manufacturers — including Phonak (Sonova Group), Signia (WS Audiology), Oticon (Demant), Starkey, and ReSound (GN Group) — implement proprietary data formats, encrypted firmware, and locked fitting software ecosystems that prevent patients from:

- Reading their own audiogram from their device
- Exporting their fitting profile in any open format
- Modifying any parameter without professional fitting software
- Transferring their fitting data between audiologists without institutional cooperation
- Accessing their own device logs or usage history

The industry justification for this lock is patient safety — the claim that self-adjustment of hearing aid parameters could cause harm.

This justification does not withstand scrutiny.

### 2.2 The safety argument examined

Hearing aids amplify sound. The parameters that govern that amplification are not dangerous in the way that, for example, insulin dosing parameters are dangerous. A person who incorrectly adjusts their compression ratio will experience discomfort or poor sound quality. They will not be harmed. They will adjust again.

The human auditory system provides immediate, continuous feedback. If a parameter is wrong, the wearer knows instantly. There is no delayed consequence, no silent failure mode, no irreversible damage pathway from standard fitting parameter adjustment.

The safety argument is not the reason for the lock.

### 2.3 The actual reason for the lock

The actual reason for the proprietary lock is the recurring revenue model of audiological healthcare.

In the United Kingdom, a private hearing aid fitting costs £150–£300. Annual aftercare contracts cost £100–£200 per year. Fine-tuning appointments — adjustments to the fitting profile that should be the patient's right to make themselves — cost £75–£150 per visit.

The lock on patient data is the mechanism that makes this revenue model function. If patients could read and adjust their own fitting profiles, they would not need to pay for appointments to do so.

The proprietary lock is not a safety feature. It is a billing mechanism.

## Part III — The legal framework

### 3.1 United Kingdom — UK GDPR

Under the UK General Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR), Article 15 provides the right of access to personal data. Audiological data — including audiograms, fitting profiles, and device usage logs — constitutes personal data under Article 4(1) as it relates to an identified natural person.

Furthermore, audiological data constitutes **special category data** under Article 9 as health data — data concerning a person's physical health condition. Special category data attracts heightened protection.

The right of access under Article 15 includes the right to receive a copy of personal data in a **commonly used, machine-readable format** where technically feasible. A proprietary binary file format stored in a hearing aid, unreadable without manufacturer software, does not satisfy this requirement.

A patient may submit a Subject Access Request (SAR) to their hearing aid manufacturer demanding:
- Their complete audiogram data
- Their fitting profile in a readable format
- Their device usage logs
- Any other personal data held in their device or the manufacturer's systems

The manufacturer must respond within one calendar month.

### 3.2 United Kingdom — Consumer Rights Act 2015

Under the Consumer Rights Act 2015, a consumer who purchases a product has the right to expect that product to be fit for purpose and to function as described. A hearing aid is described as a device to improve the wearer's hearing. The parameters governing that improvement are part of the product's function.

The argument that a consumer has no right to access or modify the operational parameters of a product they own and wear inside their body is not supported by consumer law.

### 3.3 United States — HIPAA and the 21st Century Cures Act

Under the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA), patients have the right to access their Protected Health Information (PHI), including audiological records. The 21st Century Cures Act (2016) explicitly prohibits **information blocking** — the practice of interfering with the access, exchange, or use of electronic health information.

Proprietary hearing aid data formats that prevent patients from accessing their own health data in interoperable formats constitute a form of information blocking as defined by the Act.

### 3.4 European Union — EU GDPR and the European Health Data Space

The EU GDPR provides equivalent rights to UK GDPR under Articles 15 and 20. Article 20 specifically provides the **right to data portability** — the right to receive personal data in a structured, commonly used, and machine-readable format, and to transmit that data to another controller.

The European Health Data Space (EHDS) regulation, adopted in 2024, further reinforces patient rights to access and control their own health data across EU member states.

## Part IV — The Burgess Principle applied

The Burgess Principle — established in <a href="https://github.com/ljbudgie/Burgessprinciple">the companion repository</a> — asserts that data processed without individual sovereign validation is tainted at the source and constitutes a nullity.

Applied to audiological data:

**The taint**: When a hearing aid manufacturer stores a patient's audiometric data in a proprietary format, processes it through undisclosed algorithms, and prevents the patient from accessing or verifying it — that data is processed without the sovereign validation of the data subject. The patient cannot confirm what is stored. They cannot verify it is accurate. They cannot correct it. They cannot export it.

**The nullity**: Any commercial claim, service charge, or data processing activity premised on audiological data held in a proprietary system to which the patient has been denied access is premised on tainted data. The patient's consent to that processing — given when they agreed to fitting software terms they could not read or verify — was not informed consent. It was bulk-processed consent.

**The sovereign assertion**: By using OpenHear to read their own fitting data, export their own audiogram, and adjust their own parameters — the patient is not hacking their device. They are asserting their pre-existing right to their own biometric data. The Noahlink Wireless is the tool. The assertion is the act.
