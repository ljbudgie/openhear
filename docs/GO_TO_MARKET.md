# OpenHear Go-To-Market, Mission, and Showcase Applications

---

## Mission statement

OpenHear gives people control over their own senses. It lets you choose what you hear, what you ignore, what your body learns from sound, and how your environment reaches you, whether you are deaf, hard of hearing, hearing, tired, stressed, training, sleeping, working, or simply wanting peace.

---

## Five sequential audience positionings

### 1. Deaf and hard of hearing users

OpenHear starts with the people most failed by the current system: deaf and hard of hearing users locked into expensive black-box devices and clinic-gated settings. OpenHear gives them direct ownership of their audiogram, direct control over DSP, direct environmental intelligence on the wrist, and a path toward a hearing system that is not owned by a manufacturer, a clinic, or a cloud service.

### 2. Therapeutic wellness users

Once the platform can control acoustic load and deliver evidence-led low-frequency haptics, OpenHear becomes a recovery and regulation tool for people who want better sleep, calmer work, cleaner focus, and more informed stress management. The product is not “wellness content”; it is a programmable sensory environment tied to the user's own physiology and controlled entirely on-device.

### 3. Performance athletes and professionals

For athletes, musicians, surgeons, pilots, first responders, and other precision professions, OpenHear is a selective-perception instrument. It isolates the cues that matter, suppresses noise that wastes cognition, preserves situational awareness, and creates reusable profession-specific acoustic profiles that improve performance under real conditions.

### 4. Sensory augmentation early adopters

For users drawn to new human capability, OpenHear is the first open platform that turns sound into an extensible sense rather than a passive stream. Ultrasonic cues, infrasonic warnings, anomaly detection, and full-sphere spatial awareness move the system beyond replacement into augmentation, all without surrendering control to proprietary AI.

### 5. General population acoustic-sovereignty users

The final audience is everyone who has ever wanted agency over noise: parents, commuters, remote workers, students, shift workers, caregivers, and city dwellers. OpenHear becomes the operating system for the acoustic world, letting ordinary people shape the environments they live in instead of enduring whatever reaches their ears by default.

## Showcase applications

1. **Athletics — coach isolation in a stadium.** A sprinter hears the starter and coach at full clarity while crowd noise collapses into the background. The same mode logs whether reduced acoustic load improves reaction time and stress metrics.

2. **Football / rugby — teammate and referee priority.** A player gets directional teammate shouts and referee whistles without the stadium wash. Haptic overlays preserve awareness even when line of sight is poor.

3. **Orchestral music — own instrument isolation.** A cellist hears their own instrument and section balance as if in a practice room while still tracking the conductor and key ensemble cues. A sharable orchestra profile lets other players fork and tune the same pack.

4. **Live concert attendance — performance without crowd chatter.** A concertgoer keeps the show, the venue acoustic image, and selected companions while suppressing conversations, bar noise, and hearing-aid distortion artefacts. The result is the performance, not the clutter around it.

5. **Surgery — instrument and monitor focus.** A surgeon hears vital monitor signatures, team speech, and instrument cues while theatre HVAC and peripheral noise are attenuated. The mode is tuned as a profession pack rather than a generic “speech enhancement” preset.

6. **Pilot operations — ATC above all else.** OpenHear prioritises ATC, cockpit warnings, and checklists while controlling cabin and engine noise exposure. Spatial rendering preserves directionality and urgency instead of flattening everything into one loud stream.

7. **First responder sleep — wake only for the right alarm.** A firefighter sleeps through ordinary environmental noise but wakes instantly for their station alert tone or child cry. Sleep quality and false-wake reduction are tracked against the active profile.

8. **Parenting — hear your child, not the office.** A parent working from home suppresses office noise and appliances while keeping their child's voice and distress cues at full priority. The same mode can shift automatically during bedtime and overnight monitoring.

9. **Children and development — early drift detection.** A child uses a safe, playful peak-hearing profile that reinforces speech and environmental awareness while logging repeatable changes in detection performance. Parents can export evidence for early intervention before hearing loss is formally diagnosed.

10. **Infrastructure and engineering — anomaly hearing.** An engineer receives haptic alerts when a machine, bridge, or HVAC system produces an unusual harmonic or infrasonic signature. OpenHear turns subtle acoustic drift into explicit, trainable perception.
