# OpenHear Go-To-Market, Mission, and Showcase Applications

---

## Mission statement

OpenHear gives every person control over how sound reaches them. It lets you choose what you hear, what you ignore, what your body learns from vibration, and how your environment affects your mind, whether you are deaf, hard of hearing, hearing, young, old, technical, non-technical, working, resting, training, or simply trying to live in peace.

---

## Five sequential audience positionings

### 1. Deaf and hard of hearing users

OpenHear starts with the people most failed by today's hearing system: deaf and hard of hearing users trapped inside expensive black-box hardware, clinic-gated settings, and proprietary data formats. OpenHear gives them direct ownership of their audiogram, direct control over DSP, active environmental intelligence on the wrist, and a credible path toward a hearing system that belongs to the user rather than the manufacturer.

### 2. Therapeutic wellness users

Once the wristband can deliver evidence-led 30-300 Hz protocols and reduce acoustic overload, OpenHear becomes a programmable recovery and regulation platform for people who want better sleep, calmer work, cleaner focus, and measurable physiological benefit. The value is not generic wellness content; it is a user-owned sensory environment linked to the user's own body and controlled entirely on-device.

### 3. Performance athletes and professionals

For athletes, musicians, surgeons, pilots, dispatchers, and first responders, OpenHear is a selective-perception instrument that isolates the cues that matter and suppresses the cues that waste cognition. Profession-specific acoustic profiles, haptic overlays, and biometric feedback turn hearing from a passive sense into an actively configured performance system.

### 4. Sensory augmentation early adopters

For people drawn to new human capability, OpenHear is the first open platform that extends hearing beyond biology without surrendering the experience to proprietary AI. Ultrasonic detection, infrasonic awareness, anomaly alerts, and 360° spatial rendering turn the wristband into a new sense rather than a replacement for an old product category.

### 5. The general population seeking acoustic sovereignty

The final audience is everyone who has ever wanted control over noise: parents, commuters, students, shift workers, city residents, remote workers, caregivers, and anyone exhausted by an acoustic environment they did not choose. OpenHear becomes the operating system for the acoustic world, letting ordinary people configure what reaches them instead of enduring whatever the environment imposes.

## Showcase applications

1. **Athletics — crowd filter at the starting blocks.** A sprinter hears the starter pistol and coach at full clarity while 80,000 spectators collapse into the background.
2. **Team sport — referee and teammate priority.** A footballer keeps referee whistles and teammate calls directional and clear while the stadium wash is attenuated.
3. **Concert attendance — performance without chatter.** A music fan hears the stage mix and room image while bar noise, conversations, and distortion artefacts are suppressed.
4. **Orchestral performance — own instrument isolation.** A cellist hears their own instrument and the conductor as if in a practice room while the full ensemble remains spatially intelligible.
5. **Surgery — instrument and monitor focus.** A surgeon hears team speech, instrument cues, and patient vitals while theatre HVAC and irrelevant movement noise drop away.
6. **Aviation — ATC above all else.** A pilot keeps ATC, warnings, and checklist cues at maximum priority while engine and cabin noise are managed without losing situational awareness.
7. **Parenting — sleep through traffic, wake for your child.** Sleep mode suppresses neighbours and road noise but keeps the user's child's voice or cry fully live.
8. **Therapeutic recovery — scheduled 40 Hz and low-frequency delivery.** After training, an athlete runs a wrist-based recovery protocol timed against HRV and sleep stage rather than guesswork.
9. **Engineering — anomaly hearing for infrastructure.** An engineer receives a distinct haptic alert when a bridge, pump, or HVAC system produces an unusual harmonic or infrasonic signature.
10. **Shared moments — haptic social presence.** Two OpenHear users in different places feel the same acoustic profile or send a private haptic phrase to say “I am here” without speaking.
